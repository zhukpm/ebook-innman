  • Adequacy of information
    expresses the degree to which it corresponds to the objective situation. It may be inadequate if supplemented with unreliable data. However, even complete and reliable data can be transformed into inadequate information if inadequate processing methods are applied.
  • Relevance of information:
    It expresses the degree of the information's relevance to the current moment. Often relevance, as well as completeness, is associated with commercial value of information. As information processes are stretched in time, reliable and adequate, but outdated information can lead to erroneous decisions, become irrelevant and unnecessary.
  • Reliability of information:
    reliable information that does not raise doubts. It is determined by the extent to which it fairly reflects the socio-economic and other parameters of the object in question. The degree of credibility is monitored throughout the entire process of using the information, starting from the moment it is selected.
  • Information support for solutions:
    an essential prerequisite for developing good scenarios of problem situations, determining the basis and selecting the moment of decision-making. Timely processing of reliable information, characterizing the state of interrelations of the organization with the external environment, allows making reasoned decisions.
  • Objectivity of information:
    information by which an event or phenomenon can be accurately portrayed. The level of objectivity of information decreases as it passes through communication channels. The state of affairs may be distorted to an extent that makes the decisions made on the basis of such information more or less worthless.
  • Information completeness
    is largely characterized by its quality and comprehensiveness, deemed sufficient for making appropriate innovative decisions.
  • Timeliness of information:
    means that the information reaches the user in sufficient time to enable a management decision to be made (without the risk of obsolescence). The timeliness and speed with which information reaches the user is the most important characteristic of its value. As new data becomes available, information loses its usefulness and becomes of little, if not no use at all.
  • Cost-effectiveness of information:
    the ratio of the cost of information to the results obtained from its use should be rational. Any information is costly to obtain and, as a rule, the higher the quality, the more expensive it is to collect, process and store. It is desirable that with the least amount of primary data and the least cost the information contains the maximum amount of useful information. An acceptable balance should be found between the cost and value of the information used.
